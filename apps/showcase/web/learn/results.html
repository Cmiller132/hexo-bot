The results — strength over training

This page reports the measured results of the run, read from its own records. It uses two instruments: an arena that plays frozen checkpoints against a fixed pool every five epochs and fits one pooled rating over all recorded games, and four fixed positions read by four checkpoints of the same network with the search disabled, showing the raw priors.

Ground truth. Everything below is the real shrimp_main_7 run. The figures fetch two snapshots baked from its records: data/eval_history.json (every multistage eval since epoch 5, parsed from the run’s eval diagnostics) and data/checkpoints.json (the ep2 / ep14 / ep30 / ep58 nets reading four fixed positions). Nothing on this page is illustrative or synthetic.

01the rating curve

Every five epochs the run branches off a 128-game evaluation at 512 visits. The candidate plays the current champion, a bracket of the run’s own frozen checkpoints, the final nets of two earlier runs (main5_ep105, main6_ep73), and SealBot, a scripted tactical opponent. Checkpoint matches are paired: each opening is played twice with sides swapped and scored as a pair. The pentanomial records in the readout below are those LL…WW pair histograms. Every match edge accumulates into one pooled Bradley–Terry fit (70 edges over 27 players by the latest eval), and the curve is the latest fit’s rating for each frozen checkpoint label.

SealBot is pinned at 0 to give the pool a zero point, and every number is a position within this pool of frozen opponents; the numbers are not absolute Elo and are not comparable outside the run. The verdicts are informational: the next epoch trains from the newest weights regardless of the verdict. Ten of twelve verdicts were INCONCLUSIVE, the expected outcome at a 128-game budget with confidence intervals routinely ±100 points.

The curve climbs overall with local dips. From −21 at ep5 to +231 at ep60 is about 250 points of pool-relative strength, but the middle sags: the pooled table puts ep20 at −97 and ep25 at −177, below ep5’s point estimate. Part of that is thin evidence: those labels later serve mainly as opponents (by the epoch-40 eval, ep40 beat ep20 eight pairs to none), and ep25’s interval spans [−447, +93]. Part may be a genuine mid-run trough; the intervals overlap too much to distinguish the two. Thin labels are noisy: the epoch-25 weights sit in the pool under two labels, and rate +84 as cand_ep25 but −177 as the frozen ep25, the same network rated on different games. The dip from ep45 (+150) to ep50 (+56) has the same character. The curve is accumulated evidence and moves as new games enter the pool.

02four positions, four checkpoints

The ratings measure overall strength; the positions show where it changed. The four boards below are read by four checkpoints of the run with the search disabled: the display shows the policy prior, value, 2-turn short-term value and moves-left directly from the net. At play time these priors decide which moves the 1024-visit search considers.

In the live-four position, the opponent’s stones sit at q = 1, 2, 3, 5 on the r = 1 row, a split four with its gap at (4, 1). Every live window through the four runs over that gap, so there is exactly one saving cell. ep2 misses it: 0.41 of its mass goes to (0, 1), the outside extension, after which (4, 1) and (6, 1) complete the six in a single opponent turn; the saving cell gets 0.06. ep14’s value drops to −0.90, but it still puts 0.37 on (0, 1) and a fifth of its mass on (3, 0), which defends nothing, while the save sits at 0.03. ep30 is the first to rank the gap first (0.30). ep58 puts 0.52 on (4, 1), assigns the wrong blocks near-zero mass, and reads the value at −0.15. The value signal appeared at epoch 14; the policy first ranked the save correctly sixteen epochs later, and a majority of its mass took until epoch 58.

The double-threat position has split fours on the r = 1 row and the q = −3 column, with gaps at (4, 1) and (−3, 3); the mover’s two placements this turn must cover both. ep2 gives that pair a combined 0.39 (its top move is again (0, 1)), ep14 0.71, ep30 0.85, and ep58 puts 0.99 on exactly those two cells, from the prior alone. On the 40-stone board, with one live four left (empties (−1, 2) and (1, 2)), even ep2 finds the block at 0.79; simple tactics are learned early. Training adds certainty (ep58: 0.990 on the block) and a value head that reads the position as winning (0.35 → 0.97). The quiet middlegame stays diffuse at every checkpoint; section 03 covers this.

03policy sharpening

The entropy of the policy over the legal set summarises the change. The tactical boards move toward certainty as the run ages: the live four from 2.22 to 1.46 nats, the double threat 1.87 → 1.43 → 1.16 → 0.75, the late-game block from 0.65 to 0.06, nearly a point mass. The quiet middlegame does not: 2.08, 2.02, 2.30, 1.82. It stays diffuse, and ep30 is less sharp there than ep14. The non-monotone value shows that training does not apply a global sharpening. The policy sharpens where the search kept finding forced continuations, and a quiet board with 141 legal moves and many playable plans should stay diffuse; a 99%-confident answer there would be miscalibrated.

The cause is the training target: the policy trains toward the search’s improved distribution, which is sharp where sequential halving found a forced line and flat where it did not. Sixty epochs of that target produce the change from 0.41 on the wrong block to 0.52 on the correct one. The play tab serves this same ladder of checkpoints, so the difference can be played against directly.
