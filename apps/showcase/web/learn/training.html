The training — learning from its own games

Shrimp trains only on its own games. Each epoch it plays a batch of games against itself, records what its search concluded at every decision, and trains the network to predict those conclusions and the games' final outcomes. Every few epochs a standalone evaluator measures whether the network got stronger.

01Generating experience

Training runs as a fixed loop. Each epoch: self-play generates 256 fresh games from the current checkpoint, the replay window is re-selected over everything played so far, the net trains for a single pass over a sample of that window, a checkpoint is saved (every epoch, unconditionally), and an evaluation stage runs every fifth epoch. The loop then repeats with the updated net.

Purely greedy self-play would collapse into a handful of repeated games, so three mechanisms shape the data:

  • Playout-cap randomization (PCR). A random ~33% of decisions get the full 1024-visit search; those become the rows the policy learns from. The rest run a 192-visit search, are played greedily (temperature 0), and are recorded as value-only rows. The lower cost yields more games and more diverse final outcomes for the value head, without diluting the policy targets with shallow searches.
  • Opening randomization. A quarter of games open with a few plies (average 4, at most 8) sampled straight from the net's prior at a high temperature, skipping search entirely; those init rows are never trained on. After that, moves are sampled with a play temperature that starts at 1.0 and halves every 10 plies down to a floor of 0.15, so play is exploratory early and near-deterministic late.
  • A widened candidate draw. The Gumbel root sampler picks its 32 root candidates from a softened version of the prior (draw temperature 1.5). Only the draw is softened; the ranking, the played move, and the exported target all use raw logits. The search therefore occasionally examines moves the prior underrates, which corrects the prior's blind spots.

Each placement (the opening single stone, then two per turn) is searched and recorded as its own decision, with a phase feature (first or second stone of the turn) so the net always knows which half of a turn it is choosing. Every finished game leaves two artifacts: a compact per-game .npz training shard (committed by a JSON sidecar, so a power-cut mid-write never corrupts the buffer) and an entry in the epoch's .hxr game record for replay and analysis.

02Training targets

Every full-search row stores several targets. The two primary ones:

  • The improved policy π′. The Gumbel-AlphaZero improved policy: π′ = softmax( logits + σ(completed Q) ),  σ(q) = (cvisit + maxb N(b)) · cscale · q This is the prior's logits adjusted by each candidate's search-estimated value. Candidates need at least 1 visit to enter the target's support, and the exported target uses a softened cscale of 0.35 (the search itself ranks with 1.0), because the raw target proved too sharp as a training signal. Rows without a π′ target fall back to the plain visit-count distribution, per row.
  • The outcome z. The game's final result, +1 or −1 from the mover's perspective, attached to every row of the game. The value head outputs a distribution over 65 support points spanning [−1, +1], trained with cross-entropy against a "two-hot" target that splits its mass between the two bins adjacent to z, and decoded at play time as the softmax expectation. Truncated games mask the outcome heads and still train the policy heads.

Auxiliary targets regularize the trunk: short-term values at horizons of 2, 6 and 16 turns (exponential averages of future search values, decay (m−1)/(m+1)), a less noisy, nearer-term signal than z; a moves-left head (turns until the game ends, capped at 209 and binned the same way); the opponent's reply policy; a per-move Q head; and a square-root-softened copy of π′ for a soft-policy head. In this run they are weighted 1.0 (policy), 1.0 (value), 0.25 (opponent policy), 0.1 (short-term), 0.2 (moves-left), 0.1 (Q), 1.0 (soft). Rows where the search disagreed most with the prior, measured as KL(visits‖prior), get up to 8× weight in the policy loss.

The loop. The policy head distills the search and the value head distills the outcome. What 1024 visits of search concluded in one generation becomes the next generation's zero-visit prior, and the next generation's search starts from that stronger prior. This cycle, repeated a few hundred times, is the training method.

03The shuffle window

Shards accumulate across epochs, and each epoch trains on a window over the most recent rows, KataGo-style. If the run has produced T usable rows in total, the window covers the newest

W(T) = 20,000 + 0.8 · ( x0.65 − c0.65 ) / ( 0.65 · c−0.35 ),   x = T − 20,000 + c,  c = 20,000

rows, a power-law taper. Early on the window grows nearly one-for-one with new data (0.8 new window rows per new row); by a million rows the growth rate has tapered to about 0.2, so the window keeps sliding forward but covers an ever-smaller fraction of history. The cut is made in whole per-game shards, newest first. If the window exceeds 600,000 rows, files are uniformly thinned back toward that target; from what survives, 96,000 rows are drawn for exactly one training pass. No row repeats within an epoch, and a reuse governor caps training at 8 samples per newly generated row, bounding how often stale data is revisited. Within the window, sampling is uniform; recency preference comes from the sliding cut rather than per-row weights.

The window balances two failure modes. Training only on the newest games is unstable: the data distribution shifts every epoch and openings that stop appearing are forgotten. Training on all history is stale: old games were played by a much weaker player. The taper sits between the two.

At train time every sampled row is also re-expressed under one of the hex board's 12 symmetries (6 rotations × 2 reflections, the dihedral group D6). The symmetry is drawn deterministically per row per epoch, so a row that survives in the window for 20 epochs is seen in up to 12 different orientations, a 12× data multiplier with no storage cost.

04Evaluation

Loss curves do not measure strength directly, because the loss target moves every epoch. Every fifth epoch a standalone evaluator plays the fresh checkpoint through 128 paired games at 512 visits against a small roster of earlier checkpoints: a rung from a fixed log-spaced grid of past epochs plus a reference checkpoint lagged five epochs back. Pairs share an opening (temperature-sampled, then seat-swapped), so both players face the same starts and opening luck cancels out of the comparison. Each pair scores pentanomially (0, ½, 1, 1½ or 2 points), which accounts for the correlation between a pair's two games, and every edge is pooled into a persisted Bradley-Terry rating fit across all checkpoints evaluated so far. A single 128-game epoch only resolves ~100–120 Elo; the pooled fit tightens as evidence accumulates across epochs.

An external minimax bot (SealBot) can be added as a cross-lineage reference pinned at 0 Elo, fixing the scale's zero point. It is disabled in this run, so ratings float relative to the run's own anchor checkpoints: differences between epochs are meaningful; the absolute numbers are not.

Informational verdict. The evaluator emits a PROMOTE / REGRESS / INCONCLUSIVE verdict, and nothing consumes it. Every epoch's checkpoint becomes the next epoch's self-play engine regardless. With steady learning-rate training a genuine regression shows up as a trend rather than a single bad epoch, and gating on a measurement with ~50 Elo of noise would mostly veto good checkpoints. The verdict is for the people monitoring the run. The run’s rating history, from this same eval pipeline, is on the results page.

05Summary

The net plays 256 games against itself, recording what its search concluded at every full-strength decision. It then trains on a tapered window of recent experience, learning to predict the search's improved policy and the games' eventual outcomes in a single forward pass. The new checkpoint becomes the player for the next epoch's games, so every generation's search distills into the next generation's prior. Every fifth epoch, paired matches against earlier checkpoints measure progress; the result does not gate the loop.
