The symmetry — the defining property

Every layer of hexfield_eq satisfies one constraint: transform the board by any of the 12 symmetries of the hexagonal lattice and the network’s output transforms with it — exactly, for every position and every weight the net will ever hold. This page walks that constraint through the model: the 12 symmetries and the guarantee they define; the idea that makes the guarantee cheap — each cell’s 192 trunk channels organized as 12 permuting slots of 16; and what the constraint costs each layer type: convolutions keep 84 of a nominal 1008 weight blocks, attention heads are pinned one-per-win-axis, and the position-bias table collapses to 81 free values. Every group table on this page is the one the production model is built from.

01the 12 symmetries

The hex lattice about the origin has the dihedral symmetry group D6: six rotations by multiples of 60° and six reflections. In axial coordinates the generators are 

rot60:   (q, r) → (−r, q + r)
reflect: (q, r) → (q, −q − r)

This page names the elements e, r1…r5 (rotations by 0°–300°) and s0…s5 (rotate after reflecting, sk = rotk∘reflect) — the order the exported group tables use. Two induced actions matter to the architecture:

  • the three win axes {Q, R, QR} permute among themselves — a 60° rotation 3-cycles them, a reflection transposes two and fixes one (and the 180° rotation fixes all three, reversing each);
  • the six directions around a cell permute — rotations advance the 6-cycle, reflections reverse it.

02the guarantee

For every symmetry g and every position:

f(g·board) = g·f(board)

The policy over the transformed board is exactly the transformed policy; the value is exactly unchanged. Three consequences. The training pipeline applies no symmetry augmentation — the property holds by construction, not statistically. Symmetric positions evaluate identically up to floating-point rounding. And no stored parameter is spent representing a rotated copy of a pattern another parameter already represents. The rest of this page is the constraint each layer type satisfies to make that equation hold.

03typed input

The symmetry story starts at the input. Under a symmetry, the 16 scalar planes (0–10, 41–45) stay in place — their values move with the cells but the planes don’t trade roles. The 30 axis planes (11–40) do trade roles: rotating the board 60° maps the “own_line along Q” plane onto “own_line along R”. Each of the 10 graded window quantities carries its {Q, R, QR} values as a contiguous triple, and each triple carries the 3-element axis permutation of fig 1. The input representation is therefore 16 fixed planes + 10 permuting triples — the type the stem (§08) lifts into the trunk.

04the fiber — 12 slots × 16 channels

Each cell’s 192-channel trunk vector is organized as 12 fiber slots of 16 orbit channels — one slot per group element. A symmetry g acts on the trunk’s feature field in two coupled ways:

  1. cells move to their transformed coordinates (fig 1), and
  2. inside every cell’s vector, the 12 slots permute — slot h’s 16 numbers move to slot g·h.

This is the regular representation of D6. Because the action is a pure permutation — no sign flips, no mixing — every weight constraint on this page reduces to an index-gather. The 16 orbit channels are the free learned capacity; the 12 slots are its symmetry-indexed copies.

This is the single hardest idea in the model, and the one the picture below teaches fastest. Watch the row labels: the 16 numbers that lived in slot e don’t change — they just move to whichever slot the group multiplication sends them to. Rotations shuffle rotations among themselves and reflections among themselves; a reflection trades the two halves.

05weight tying

Convolutions. The 7-tap hex conv (center + 6 direction taps, each tap its own 192×192 matrix — direction-typed, because line direction is the content of the game) must satisfy, for every g and tap t:

W[g·t] = M(g) · W[t] · M(g)⁻¹

where g·t is the permuted tap and M(g) the slot permutation of fig 4. Solving this leaves 84 free 16×16 blocks per conv — the module stores exactly those (as w_base) and materializes the dense (7, 192, 192) weight each forward through a precomputed index-gather. The GPU kernels consume the dense weight and are unaware of the tie; under inference the materialized weights are cached and regenerate only when the parameters change, so the tie costs essentially nothing at serve time. The conv bias is slot-constant: one 16-vector broadcast over the 12 slots.

1×1 linears (attention projections, MLPs, token reads) are the center-tap special case: 12 free 16×16 blocks each. Norms are already almost free: LayerNorm’s mean/variance over all 192 channels is permutation-invariant, and the affine is tied — one (γ, β) pair per orbit channel, broadcast over the slots; LayerScale’s per-channel scale is tied identically.

06heads = win axes

The attention reshape splits 192 channels into heads × head_dim. Because the slots permute under symmetry, a head’s channels land in another head’s channels — consistent only if the head partition follows the group structure. The partition used is the three cosets of the order-4 subgroup that preserves the Q axis: 3 heads of dimension 64, one per win axis (4 slots × 16 channels each), permuted by the symmetries exactly as the axes are. The head count is structural — the build rejects any other value.

07the joint bias table

Attention adds a learned bias per query/key pair, indexed by their coordinate offset through a fixed row map: 217 rows for every exact offset within distance 8, 8 + 8 ring rows for distances 9–16 split into on-win-axis and off-axis, 1 far row, and 3 token rows (token↔cell, cell↔token, token↔token) — 237 rows, 3 head columns.

The symmetry constraint ties this table jointly across (offset-row, head) pairs: a symmetry moves the offset to a new row and the head to a new head simultaneously, so the free parameters are the orbits of that joint action — 81 free values per attention block, gathered into the full 237×3 table each forward. Head diversity survives exactly where geometry permits: an offset on no reflection axis keeps 3 independent head values; an offset on a mirror line keeps 2 (on a win axis, its own head stays free and the other two are tied; midway between axes, the two swapped heads tie); the zero offset, the ring rows, the far row and the token rows keep 1 (all heads equal). Of the 217 exact rows, 144 keep 3, 72 keep 2 (48 on win axes + 24 mid-edge), and 1 — the zero offset — keeps 1.

08tokens, reads, and the stem

  • Summary tokens — the 6 global scratchpad vectors riding in the attention sequence — carry the invariant type: a learned 6 × 16 parameter broadcast identically across all 12 slots, so symmetries fix them.
  • Output reads. A policy logit must be an invariant scalar attached to a moving cell; the value must be fully invariant. Both use group-pooling: average the 12 slots, leaving an invariant vector of one value per orbit channel. To avoid a 16-wide bottleneck, reads are widened first by a tied expansion — ×4 before pooling for the scalar heads (64-wide pooled blocks), ×2 for the per-cell heads (32-wide).
  • The stem maps the typed 46-plane input (fig 3) into the regular fiber. Scalar planes may only write slot-constant weight columns; each axis triple lifts along the coset structure of fig 6. Implemented as a free weight passed through a Reynolds projection — averaging over all 12 symmetry-conjugates — which projects onto the constraint-satisfying subspace.
