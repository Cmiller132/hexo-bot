The search

The network answers a position in one forward pass: a policy over moves and a value estimate. The search is what turns that guess into a decision. It is a policy-improvement operator: spend a budget of look-aheads, let measured outcomes correct the network's first impressions, and come back with a move choice — and a training target — that is reliably better than the raw policy alone.

Honestly stated, the engine underneath is a fairly classical Monte-Carlo tree search: a tree of positions, edges with visit counts and running values, batched network evaluations at the leaves, PUCT-style selection machinery with first-play urgency and progressive widening. What Hexo trains and serves, though, is the Gumbel AlphaZero configuration (Danihelka et al. 2022) layered on that substrate: a Gumbel-Top-m sample picks the root candidates, sequential halving splits the visit budget among them, and a deterministic visit-regularized rule replaces PUCT at interior nodes. The classic exploration tricks — Dirichlet noise at the root, forced playouts — were deliberately removed from this codebase; the Gumbel machinery is the exploration story.

Gumbel-Top-m at the root

The root of each search does not consider every legal move — on an unbounded board that would smear a finite budget over hundreds of cells. Instead it samples m candidates without replacement using the Gumbel-max trick: draw independent noise g(a) ~ Gumbel(0,1) for every legal move and keep the top m by logit(a)/τ + g(a). That comparator is exactly equivalent to sampling m moves, one after another without replacement, from softmax(logits/τ) — strong moves are picked often, weak moves are picked occasionally, and no move is ever starved of probability. The draw temperature τ = 1.5 widens only this draw: it flattens which candidates get sampled, but the halving scores, the played move, and the exported training target all read the raw logits — τ changes who gets auditioned, never how the audition is judged.

This is why Dirichlet root noise could be deleted. Classic AlphaZero perturbs the prior and then lets PUCT spend most of the budget on the (noisy) favorite anyway; the exploration is smeared and mostly wasted. Here the randomness lives in which m moves enter the tournament, and the entire visit budget is then concentrated on those m — every candidate gets enough visits for its measured value to actually mean something. In the main training run m = 32; when a search runs with a smaller budget (evaluation matches, the live site's smaller bots), m is automatically walked down the halving ladder until every round-0 candidate can afford at least 4 visits, and never below a 2-candidate tournament.

Sequential halving

The visit budget n for the move is then run as a knockout tournament over the m candidates, in R = ⌈log₂ m⌉ rounds. Each round, every surviving candidate receives an equal quota of ⌊n / (R · |Aᵣ|)⌋ fresh visits (at least one). When all survivors have met their quota — a real barrier: the round cannot advance while any evaluation is still in flight — they are ranked and the worse half is eliminated, keeping ⌈|Aᵣ|/2⌉, until one remains. The final survivor absorbs whatever budget is left. The ranking score is

g(a) + logit(a) + σ(q̂(a))   where   σ(q) = (c_visit + max₂ N(b)) · c_scale · q

with c_visit = 50, c_scale = 1.0, and max₂ N(b) the largest visit count any root move earned this move (on a reused tree, inherited visits are subtracted out so stale counts can't inflate σ). Here q̂ is the completed Q: a candidate that has visits reports its measured average; one that doesn't yet gets a principled interpolation of the node's own value estimate and the visit-weighted average of its searched siblings, rather than a lucky default. The shape of σ is the point: at 20-odd visits the multiplier is already ≈70×, so a fraction of a pawn's worth of measured value difference dwarfs any logit gap. The prior and the noise decide who enters; measured Q rapidly decides who survives.

Compare that to what PUCT does with the same budget: the prior favorite soaks up the majority of visits, and a low-prior move that happens to be tactically decisive may see single-digit visits — too few for its Q to be trusted, so it is never promoted. Sequential halving spends the budget where the decision needs it: equally at first, then doubling down only on moves that survive scrutiny. A "hidden gem" — low prior, genuinely strong — only has to out-measure half the field for one round to keep earning visits.

Threat-space search

A sampled candidate set has one frightening failure mode: what if the one move that blocks a four simply isn't drawn? Hexo closes that hole with a deterministic tactical scanner that runs alongside the search. It watches the board's length-6 windows and flags any active window — all one colour — holding 4 or more stones. From those windows it derives, phase-aware, the tactical set: the cells that complete an own win this turn, plus every empty cell of every opponent threat window. Defense is a hitting set, not fill-every-gap: one stone in any empty of a threat window kills it, and if no B-stone hitting set covers all opponent windows (B = 2 on the turn's first stone, 1 otherwise), the position is scored as a proven loss outright.

At the root, every legal tactical cell is force-included in the candidate set — appended on top of the m budget, not counted against it, even if its logit+noise score ranked far below the top-m. So the draw can never miss the blocking move. The same scanner also injects tactical cells as guaranteed children at every expanded node, overrides leaf values with proven win/loss verdicts, and vetoes a proven-losing move at the final pick.

Serving many games at once

None of the above would be affordable if each game ran its search alone: a single tree produces leaf positions one at a time, and a network evaluated one position at a time leaves the device almost idle. Hexo's driver is a continuous batched scheduler. Every active game owns a slot with its own tree; each pass, every slot descends its tree and emits leaf positions until it has up to virtual_batch_size evaluations in flight (48 during training, 32 on this site) — a virtual loss temporarily marks each in-flight path down so parallel descents spread instead of piling onto one line. All slots' leaves land in a shared queue, which is flushed to the network as one large batch; results fan back, back up their trees, and the slots immediately produce more work. Duplicate positions in a batch are evaluated once, and a bounded FIFO cache (262,144 entries, keyed by a 64-bit hash of the position) means a re-visited position costs a lookup instead of a forward pass.

Two more economies matter. When a move is played, the tree isn't discarded: the chosen child is promoted to be the new root, keeping its whole subtree of visits — each new move then gets a fresh Gumbel draw over the promoted root, and the halving quotas count only visits earned since, so reuse never distorts the tournament. And because the sequential-halving barrier only fires when a slot has zero evaluations in flight, correctness survives all this asynchrony: rounds never advance on half-backed-up statistics. Training runs 192 games per device this way; the public site uses the same scheduler, which is why several visitors can each play a searched opponent on one box.

What comes out of a search

Two artifacts leave every finished search. The played move: at temperature zero — every serious move on this site — the pick starts from the argmax of this move's visit histogram, but a lower-confidence-bound rule gets the last word: among moves with enough fresh visits (at least 8, and at least 10% of the leader's), it plays the one with the best Q − z·σ/√n at z = 1.6 — preferring a well-measured good move over a barely-sampled great-looking one — with a decisiveness tie-break among near-equals and the tactical guard vetoing any proven loser. (During training's few opening plies at T > 0, the histogram is sampled instead, with candidates that never survived a halving pruned out first — their visits were schedule, not endorsement.) And the training target: not the visit counts, but the improved policy π′(a) = softmax(logits + σ(completedQ)), a denser, better-calibrated signal than counts alone — softened for export (c_scale = 0.35) so the network isn't trained on near-one-hot spikes. Where π′ goes next is the subject of the training.
