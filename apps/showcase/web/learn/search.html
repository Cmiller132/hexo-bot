The search

The network evaluates a position in one forward pass: a policy over moves and a value estimate. The search is a policy-improvement operator: it spends a budget of look-aheads, corrects the network's estimates with measured outcomes, and returns a move choice and a training target that improve on the raw policy.

The engine is a Monte-Carlo tree search: a tree of positions, edges with visit counts and running values, batched network evaluations at the leaves, and PUCT-style selection machinery with first-play urgency and progressive widening. The trained and served configuration is Gumbel AlphaZero (Danihelka et al. 2022) on that substrate: a Gumbel-Top-m sample picks the root candidates, sequential halving splits the visit budget among them, and a deterministic visit-regularized rule selects at interior nodes.

Gumbel-Top-m at the root

An unbounded board can have hundreds of legal moves at the root, and a finite budget spread over all of them would give each too few visits to measure. The root instead samples m candidates without replacement using the Gumbel-max trick: draw independent noise g(a) ~ Gumbel(0,1) for every legal move and keep the top m by logit(a)/τ + g(a). This comparator is equivalent to sampling m moves without replacement from softmax(logits/τ): strong moves are picked often, weak moves occasionally, and every move has nonzero probability. The draw temperature τ = 1.5 applies to only this draw: it flattens the candidate sampling, while the halving scores, the played move, and the exported training target all read the raw logits.

Root exploration comes from the draw: the randomness is in which m moves enter the tournament, and the entire visit budget is then concentrated on those m, so every candidate receives enough visits for its measured value to be informative. In the main training run m = 32; when a search runs with a smaller budget (evaluation matches, the live site's smaller bots), m is automatically reduced until every round-0 candidate can receive at least 4 visits, with a floor of 2 candidates.

Sequential halving

The visit budget n for the move is then run as a knockout tournament over the m candidates, in R = ⌈log₂ m⌉ rounds. Each round, every surviving candidate receives an equal quota of ⌊n / (R · |Aᵣ|)⌋ fresh visits (at least one). When all survivors have met their quota (the round cannot advance while any evaluation is still in flight), they are ranked and the worse half is eliminated, keeping ⌈|Aᵣ|/2⌉, until one remains. The final survivor absorbs whatever budget is left. The ranking score is

g(a) + logit(a) + σ(q̂(a))   where   σ(q) = (c_visit + max₂ N(b)) · c_scale · q

with c_visit = 50, c_scale = 1.0, and max₂ N(b) the largest visit count any root move earned this move (on a reused tree, inherited visits are subtracted out so stale counts cannot inflate σ). Here q̂ is the completed Q: a candidate with visits reports its measured average; one without visits gets an interpolation of the node's own value estimate and the visit-weighted average of its searched siblings. At around 20 visits the σ multiplier is already ≈70, so a small measured value difference outweighs any logit gap. The prior and the noise decide which candidates enter; measured Q decides which survive.

The schedule spends the budget equally at first, then concentrates on moves that survive ranking. A low-prior move with a strong measured Q only has to out-measure half the field each round to keep earning visits.

One budget, two searches

The two eras structure the same budget differently, and the difference is visible on one position. The figure below runs the real thing once per side, through the same SearchProfile invocation the site serves bots with: the shrimp_main_7 profile (Gumbel root + sequential halving, ep70 net) and the shrimp_main_5 profile (PUCT root, that run's ep105 net), same quiet middlegame, 512 visits each. The Gumbel side spreads its budget over the calibrated 16-candidate set in the schedule's tiers — every candidate gets at least 8 measured visits, the two finalists 120 each — then picks between well-measured finalists. The PUCT side grows one tree greedily from the prior: the leader absorbs 229 visits, only 7 moves receive any at all, and the search stopped itself 185 visits short of the budget once the remaining budget could not overtake the visit leader (and the LCB pick agreed). Neither shape is free: SH buys a calibrated comparison across a candidate set, PUCT buys depth on the line it already believes. The two sides also disagree on the move here; note they are different networks as well as different searches, so this is a portrait of two served bots, not a controlled search-only ablation.

Threat-space search

A sampled candidate set can fail to include the one move that blocks a four. A deterministic tactical scanner runs alongside the search to close that gap. It scans the board's length-6 windows and flags any active window (all one colour) holding 4 or more stones. From those windows it derives the phase-aware tactical set: the cells that complete an own win this turn, plus every empty cell of every opponent threat window. Defense is computed as a hitting set: one stone in any empty of a threat window neutralizes it, and if no B-stone hitting set covers all opponent windows (B = 2 on the turn's first stone, 1 otherwise), the position is scored as a proven loss.

At the root, every legal tactical cell is force-included in the candidate set: appended in addition to the m budget, regardless of its logit+noise rank. The draw therefore cannot miss the blocking move. The same scanner also injects tactical cells as guaranteed children at every expanded node, overrides leaf values with proven win/loss verdicts, and vetoes a proven-losing move at the final pick.

The batched scheduler

A single tree produces leaf positions one at a time, and evaluating one position at a time leaves the device mostly idle. Shrimp's driver is a continuous batched scheduler. Every active game has a slot with its own tree; each pass, every slot descends its tree and emits leaf positions until it has up to virtual_batch_size evaluations in flight (48 during training, 32 on this site). A virtual loss temporarily lowers each in-flight path's value so parallel descents spread across different lines. All slots' leaves land in a shared queue, which is flushed to the network as one large batch; results fan back, back up their trees, and the slots immediately produce more work. Duplicate positions in a batch are evaluated once, and a bounded FIFO cache (262,144 entries, keyed by a 64-bit hash of the position) means a re-visited position costs a lookup instead of a forward pass.

When a move is played, the chosen child is promoted to be the new root and keeps its whole subtree of visits. Each new move gets a fresh Gumbel draw over the promoted root, and the halving quotas count only visits earned since promotion, so reuse does not distort the tournament. The sequential-halving barrier fires only when a slot has zero evaluations in flight, so rounds never advance on partially backed-up statistics. Training runs 192 games per device this way; the public site uses the same scheduler, so several visitors can each play a searched opponent on one device.

Search outputs

Every finished search produces two artifacts. The first is the played move. At temperature zero (how this site plays), the pick starts from the argmax of this move's visit histogram, then a lower-confidence-bound rule refines it: among moves with enough fresh visits (at least 8, and at least 10% of the leader's), it plays the one with the best Q − z·σ/√n at z = 1.6. This prefers a well-measured move over a lightly sampled one with a higher point estimate. A decisiveness tie-break applies among near-equals, and the tactical guard vetoes any proven loser. During training's few opening plies at T > 0, the histogram is sampled instead; candidates that never survived a halving round are pruned first, since their visits came from round quotas rather than survival. The second artifact is the training target: the improved policy π′(a) = softmax(logits + σ(completedQ)), a denser signal than visit counts alone. It is softened for export (c_scale = 0.35) so the network does not train on near-one-hot spikes. The training describes how π′ is used.
