\section{Computable relevance zones}
\label{sec:computable-zones}

The soundness theorem can be read as an explicit searched-set formula at each
AND node.  Its terms separate current tactics, certificate witnesses,
defender completions, and frontier reachability.

\begin{theorem}[Zone reading]
\label{thm:T4}
This statement has the same boundary as \cref{thm:T3}: it applies to valid
zone-carrying certificates obeying \cref{def:D9} and satisfying
\cref{zone:Z1,zone:Z2,zone:Z4,zone:Z5}, with exact $D_N$ and the full, rather
than sharpened, defender-placement budget.  At an AND node, the sound
searched set is
\[
\begin{split}
  Z(N)=\Legal(P_N)\cap\bigl[{}&\operatorname{hitting}(P_N)\cup\Prot(N)\\
    &{}\cup B\bigl(8D_N;
      \Prot(N)\setminus(\Legal(P_N)\cup\Stones(P_N))
    \bigr)\bigr],
\end{split}
\]
where $\operatorname{hitting}(P)$ is the union of the empty sets of the
$\A$-threat windows of $P$, namely the cells in the members of $\hit(P)$.
The three terms are the hitting cells, the protected cells consisting of the
core and completion-guard empties, and the horizon-scaled frontier band.

The completion guard counts all defender plies before the horizon.  The
sharpened version that counts only non-hit placements is not proved; it is
listed in \cref{sec:open-problems}.  The frontier band is empty whenever
$\Prot(N)\subseteq\Legal(P_N)\cup\Stones(P_N)$.  This is the typical case,
since witness windows cluster near the action.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The three terms are exactly the searched cells required by
\cref{zone:Z1,zone:Z2,zone:Z5}.  With \cref{zone:Z4} and the certificate
grammar, \cref{thm:T3} proves that dismissing every other legal reply is
sound.
\end{proof}

For short threat-creating continuations, future attacker placements remain
inside windows already touched by current attacker stones.

\begin{lemma}[Short-range attacker-core containment]
\label{lem:L10}
Suppose every attacker placement in a certificate is threat-creating: it
lands in a window that, immediately before the placement, is alive for
$\A$ and contains at least three attacker stones.  If a placement is the
attacker's $k$th future placement from a node $N$, then for $k\le3$ its
window contains at least
\[
  3-(k-1)\ge1
\]
attacker stones already present in $P_N$.  Thus the placement cell lies in
an $\A$-touched window that is alive for $\A$ at $P_N$.  This statement covers attacker-placement core
cells; named witness windows are a separate, certificate-known component of
the core.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The window has at least three attacker stones at placement time.  At most
$k-1$ of them can be future stones, one from each earlier future attacker
placement.  At least $3-(k-1)$ are therefore current at $N$, which is
positive for $k\le3$.  Since the window is alive immediately before the
placement, backward permanence from \cref{lem:L4} makes it alive at $P_N$.
\end{proof}

From the fourth future attacker placement onward, an attacker core cell may
lie in a currently virgin window.  Such setup cells must be supplied
explicitly from the certificate core.

A common radius-three generator covers the full zone only under a joint set
of short-horizon and witness-location hypotheses.

\begin{theorem}[Static-set coverage for short horizons]
\label{thm:T5}
\upshape
This is a coverage theorem under all of the following joint hypotheses:
$D_N\le3$; at most three remaining attacker placements are made; those
placements are threat-creating; every named witness-window cell is in an
$\A$-touched window that is alive for $\A$ at $P_N$; and the frontier band
is empty.  Let
\[
  r_3(P)=\{c\in\Legal(P):
    \exists s\in\Stones(P),\ \dist(c,s)\le3\}.
\]
Then
\[
  r_3(P_N)\ \cup\
  \{\textup{empty cells of $\A$-touched alive windows at $P_N$}\}
\]
covers $Z(N)$.

Without all of these hypotheses, the searched set must include
$\operatorname{core}(\mathcal C,N)\cap\Legal(P_N)$ explicitly.  The static
set is then a candidate generator, not a certified zone by itself.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $D_N\le3$, a completion-guard window has at least three defender stones,
so each of its empty cells is within distance $3$ of a defender stone by
\cref{lem:L1}.  Hitting cells are within radius $2$ by \cref{thm:T1}.
Under the stated limit on the remaining threat-creating placements,
\cref{lem:L10} puts attacker-placement core cells in $\A$-touched alive
windows.  The separate witness hypothesis does the same for named witness cells, and
the last hypothesis removes the frontier term.  These observations cover
every term of the formula in \cref{thm:T4}.
\end{proof}

When every defender turn is already fully occupied by threat hits, the
completion and frontier guards can be removed through a local auxiliary
refutation.

\begin{theorem}[Forced-tree regime]
\label{thm:T6}
\upshape
Let $\mathcal C$ be a valid certificate under \cref{def:D9}.  Suppose every
internal AND node with defender budget $b$ has verifier-checked
$\mhs(P_N)=b$, and the defender's immediate-win predicate is false.  Then the
sufficient searched set is
\[
  S(N)=\operatorname{hitting}(P_N)\cup
       \bigl(\operatorname{core}(\mathcal C,N)\cap\Legal(P_N)\bigr).
\]
The completion-guard and frontier terms are unnecessary.  The auxiliary
refutation used in the proof is measured against the same certified horizon
$T$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Work at the first dismissed reply $d$.  Before it, the real play follows
certificate lines exactly.  Let $b$ be the defender budget at its node.
Since every hitting cell is searched, $d$ is non-hitting.  Attach an
auxiliary refutation according to $b$.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)}]\upshape
  \item If $b=2$, the successor remains a defender node with budget $b'=1$.
    The untouched threat family still needs two hits, so its hitting number
    is greater than $b'$.  It supplies a defender LOSS leaf with the current
    threat family as its witness family.
  \item If $b=1$, the successor has the attacker to move with budget $2$ and
    at least one surviving attacker threat.  This supplies an attacker WIN
    leaf: a count-$4$ witness at budget $2$, or a count-$5$ witness, satisfies
    $\ownwinnow$.
\end{enumerate}

The auxiliary leaf witnesses are the node's current attacker-threat windows.
Their empty cells are hitting cells of the node and are covered by the
searched set.  Thus the auxiliary branch needs no additional appeal to the
original subtree's core.

Its resolution fits inside the original horizon $T$.  Had the defender used
a searched minimum hitting reply instead, a pair when $b=2$ or a common hit
when $b=1$, every current attacker threat would be killed; defender
placements cannot create a new attacker threat.  The original certificate
therefore cannot resolve on the first following attacker ply.  Its horizon
already reaches the second attacker ply, which is exactly what the auxiliary
branch needs: $\text{leaf-ply}+b'+2$ in the $b=2$ case, and the attacker's
own turn in the $b=1$ case.

It remains to rule out defender completion before the auxiliary resolution.
Because the defender's immediate-win predicate is checked as false at each
AND node, a defender-alive window has defender count at most $3$ when $b=2$,
and at most $4$ when $b=1$.
Indeed, count $4$ at budget $2$, or count $5$ at either budget, would satisfy
$\ownwinnow$.  When $b=2$, the dismissal and the single placement in the
$b'=1$ LOSS contract contribute two defender placements in total; the latter
placement is counted once.  The count reaches at most $3+2=5$.  When $b=1$,
only the dismissal precedes the attacker's turn, so the count reaches at
most $4+1=5$.  No defender window completes before the attacker resolution.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The relevance closure}

The implementable closure uses only touched-window queries and falls back to
the full legal set when a finite touched-window representation no longer
covers every completion guard.

\begin{definition}[Horizon relevance closure]
\label{def:D13}
Let $P$ have the defender to move, and let
$D=\DefCount(P,T)$ be the number of defender placements before the horizon.
Define
\[
  \mathcal A(P)=
  \{\text{empties of alive windows with $\cnt_\A\ge1$ and $\cnt_\D=0$}\}
\]
and
\[
  \mathcal G(P,D)=
  \{\text{empties of $\D$-alive windows with $\cnt_\D\ge6-D$}\}.
\]
Then
\[
  R(P,D)=
  \begin{cases}
    \Legal(P)\cap
      [\operatorname{hitting}(P)\cup\mathcal A(P)\cup\mathcal G(P,D)],
      &D\le5,\\[1mm]
    \Legal(P),&D\ge6.
  \end{cases}
\]
For $D\le5$, $\mathcal G(P,D)$ is a finite touched-window query.

The set $R$ is a candidate generator, not a certified zone by itself.
The set $\mathcal A$ covers attacker core cells only within the range of
\cref{lem:L10}; deeper setup cells may lie outside every term of $R$.
Certified sufficiency uses
\[
  R(P_N,D_N)
  \cup(\operatorname{core}(\mathcal C,N)\cap\Legal(P_N))
  \cup\bigl[\Legal(P_N)\cap B\bigl(8D_N;
    \Prot(N)\setminus(\Legal(P_N)\cup\Stones(P_N))
  \bigr)\bigr].
\]
The extra terms are certificate-known and verifier-enumerable.  The
$D\ge6$ fallback is conservative.  The soundness theorem can still dismiss
deep-interior cells at that depth when they lie in no defender-alive window
and are outside the hitting, core, and frontier terms.  Lying in no
defender-alive window is necessary but not sufficient for dismissal.  Every
certified-sufficiency claim also presupposes a valid certificate under
\cref{def:D9} and a separate verification of \cref{zone:Z4}.
\end{definition}

The closure plus the two certificate-derived additions covers each
verifier-required zone term.

\begin{theorem}[Closure and certificate-extras coverage]
\label{thm:T7}
\upshape
This is a coverage theorem.  Searching at every AND node the set
\[
\begin{split}
  R(P_N,D_N)
  &\cup(\operatorname{core}(\mathcal C,N)\cap\Legal(P_N))\\
  &\cup\bigl[\Legal(P_N)\cap B\bigl(8D_N;
    \Prot(N)\setminus(\Legal(P_N)\cup\Stones(P_N))
  \bigr)\bigr]
\end{split}
\]
satisfies \cref{zone:Z1,zone:Z2,zone:Z5}.  Certified dismissal soundness
additionally presupposes a valid certificate under \cref{def:D9}, separate
verification of \cref{zone:Z4}, exact $D_N$, and the full defender-placement
budget.

Both certificate terms are verifier-enumerable.  The legal part of the core
is usually redundant, for example when the next designated attacker move is
covered by \cref{lem:L10}, rather than empty.  The band is empty whenever all
protected territory is already legal or occupied.  Computing $R$ takes one
  window-store pass per node, or $O(\#\textup{touched windows})$ time.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The hitting term of $R$ gives \cref{zone:Z1}.  For $D_N\le5$, its guard term
contains the completion-guard part of $\Prot(N)$, while $\mathcal A(P_N)$
covers the short-range attacker cells described by \cref{lem:L10}; the
explicit legal core supplies every remaining protected certificate cell.
For $D_N\ge6$, $R$ is the full legal set.  The displayed band is exactly the
set required by \cref{zone:Z5}.  Hence the union satisfies the three stated
conditions, and \cref{thm:T3,thm:T4} give sound dismissal once the remaining
hypotheses are verified.
\end{proof}

The practical solver loop uses $R$ as a heuristic candidate generator.  When
a proof is found, the verifier recomputes \cref{zone:Z1,zone:Z2,zone:Z4,zone:Z5}
exactly at every node against the actual certificate, including its core and
frontier band.  A violation yields \textsc{unknown}.  Search trimming can
therefore affect completeness, but it cannot create a false verified win.

On random midgame positions, the generator has measured a mean of $147$ cells
against $302$ legal cells, with smaller sets in forced regions.  In the
fully-forcing regime of \cref{thm:T6}, the defender set consists of hitting
cells and the certificate's own legal core cells at any depth.  For
$D\ge6$, the completion guard covers every legal cell in a defender-alive
window, leaving only interior cells in no such window as possible
dismissals, subject also to the hitting, core, and frontier conditions.  This
$D\ge6$ boundary is a theorem rather than an experimental observation.
